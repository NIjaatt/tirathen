The Free Tribes are a nation located in far north-western [[Valloryn]]. The Free Tribes are represented by a winged dire wolf mid-howl.

### History
#### Pre-Humans
The harsh conditions shaped the peoples of the Frozen North into some of the most physically capable and practically knowledgeable people on Valloryn for the time period. A Northerner of the Age of Legend could read weather patterns in cloud formations, navigate by star and wind across the featureless tundra, treat a wound with materials foraged from the frozen earth, and bring down an animal three times their size. These were not exceptional skills, they were necessary ones.

Life in the Frozen North has been organized almost entirely around the cycle of seasons, which are effectively two: the long winter, and the brief summer. Winter is the time of stillness and interior life. Tribes draw inward to their winter settlements, living off what had been stored and preserved through the warmer months. It is the season of storytelling, craft and spiritual practice. The long dark nights are when oral histories are passed down, tools and weapons are made and repaired, and spiritual leaders perform the rites of the Old Faith. The hearth is not merely a heat source but the symbolic heart of a tribe's community. Letting a fire go out is considered a serious ill omen in most tribal traditions.

Conversely, summer is the time of furious, almost manic activity. The brief window of warmth and light is when hunting, fishing, foraging, and raiding all happens at maximum intensity. Every able-bodied person of a tribe, man, woman and child has a role in the summer. The volume of what can be taken and preserved in these months determine whether the tribe could survive the following winter. Children as young as five or six accompany adults on fishing trips and foraging expeditions. By twelve or thirteen, most Northern children are expected to contribute meaningfully to the tribe's survival work.

The peoples of the Frozen North have always existed in a state of low-level warfare. Different tribes competed viciously for hunting grounds, coastal fishing access, and shelter from the worst of the northern freeze. Alliances formed and collapsed along lines of convenience. There was no concept of a shared Northern identity, only tribe and kin.

Communal resource sharing was nearly universal. For a tribe that allowed its members to hoard individually would lose people to starvation in bad winters. Such focus on resources lead to inter-tribe disputes. War between different tribes was mostly low-level: raid and counter-raid over resources, the occasional ambush of a hunting party straying too close to disputed territory. Outright war was rarer, expensive in lives neither side could afford to lose, and were typically settled through negotiated terms before it escalated too far.

#### The Spellplague and Age of Darkness
The Northern peoples were not untouched by the catastrophes that reshaped Tirathen. When the Spellplague spread across the lands following the arrival of humans in 1202 P.D., the Frozen North lost roughly eight in ten of their people. Tribes that had numbered in the hundreds were reduced to dozens. Some were wiped out entirely, their names surviving only in the oral histories of neighbours who had raided them a generation earlier. The recovery was slow and painful. The North's harsh conditions made rebuilding population harder than in more hospitable lands. There were no great cities to consolidate survivors into, no agricultural surplus to feed a recovering population through lean years. Recovery happened tribe by tribe, family by family.

The Age of Darkness that followed the Great Collapse in 1808 P.D added another layer of suffering. Five hundred years of sunlight blocked by the shattered remnants of [[Belegyr]] drove temperatures across Tirathen down sharply, and the Frozen North, already the coldest inhabited region on Valloryn, became genuinely uninhabitable across much of its northern extent. Tribes abandoned the deep north entirely, compressing southward. When the sun returned in 500 AC, those northernmost territories were resettled only gradually, and some were never reclaimed.

#### The Freeze
The first signs came in the form of a winter that simply did not end when it should have. Hunters who had spent their lives reading the land noticed that the thaw was running late, and then later still, and then not arriving at all before the cold reasserted itself. The summers were becoming shorter than any in living memory. Bad seasons happened, most elders had stories of bad seasons.

It was around fifth short summer that the pattern stopped feeling like misfortune and started feeling like something else. The game herds began shifting south earlier in the season than their centuries-old patterns would predict, following warmth that was retreating faster than they could track. Fish runs thinned. The deep boreal forests, which had always recovered from hard winters, began showing signs that they were not recovering. Dead timber that should have been replaced by new growth simply stood where it fell, and new growth did not come. The cold was not merely visiting longer. The northerners, despite living in the lands for thousands of years, were unable to withstand the encroaching cold. As a result, the tribes were forced to begin trekking south towards warmer lands, in an event known as [[the Great Northern Exodus]].

By 1336 AC, more and more northerners were trekking southbound, trying to escape into the warm lands of [[Korvia]], much to their annoyance and displeasure. [[Korvia]] sent a large number of forces to the border to secure it, and keep the Northerners out. Some Northerners resisted, and made it through, others didn't make it. The rest saw they could go no further south and either returned to their homes or camped next the border forming a massive refuge camp.

#### Formation of a Nation
The refugee camps along Korvia's northern border were not a place where great things were supposed to happen. They were a place where people waited to die slowly, or waited to be turned back north to die faster. Tribes that had raided each other for generations found themselves camped within shouting distance of one another, sharing the same frozen ground and the same view of Korvian soldiers who looked through them as though they were not there. Old grudges had nowhere to breathe. Disputes over space, over food, over whose children ate first, erupted constantly and sometimes turned bloody. The camps were a pressure vessel, and the pressure built steadily. This pressure gave rise to an orcish man, who would change the course of the Frozen North's history, for better or worse.

Valdris was not the most powerful chieftain in the camps. He was not the largest, or the most feared, or the one with the most warriors at his back. What distinguished him was that he understood what was actually happening: the tribes were doing Korvia's work for them. He began by walking into disputes that were not his and offered resolutions as a practical third party. He shared his tribe's resources publicly with peoples his own tribe had historically despised. He talked to many, but listened more.

The ideology he put forward was not new. Versions of it had been said before by people who lacked the moment or the audience. The freeze does not care which tribe you belong to. Korvia does not see tribes, only savages. The south is the enemy. What made the argument land where it never had before were the Korvian soldiers standing in plain sight, and the bodies of those who had tried to push through individually and hadn't made it. Unity had always been an abstract idea but Valdris made it seem like it was not abstract at all.

Valdris's unification was slow. He would speak with a chieftain, and hear them, forming a truce. Then he would speak and hear another. He would hear the tales of two tribes with a decades-old blood feud, and worked with them to suspend their hostilities, not out of forgiveness but because of the realisation that they were not the real enemy. By the time a formal pact was on the table, most of the significant leaders in the camps had already been bound into it through a web of smaller agreements Valdris had spent months quietly assembling.

The first pact of the Free Tribes was spoken aloud under the shadow of Korvian fortification, soldiers watching from the walls above. Valdris had become High Chieftain of something that had not existed in the Frozen North's history, and the south had created a unified enemy on their doorstep.

### Geography
The Free Tribes are located in north-western Valloryn. It is bordered to the south by the [[Korvia|Korvian Empire]], and to the southeast is [[Valdyr|the Kingdom of Valdyr]].

The Frozen North is dominated by vast open tundra and flatlands swept by constant wind, broken occasionally by dense, ice-choked forests and frozen river valleys.

Further north the land rises into glacial mountain ranges, impassable for most of the year. The western and eastern coasts are rugged and rocky, bitten by cold seas that nonetheless remain navigable for experienced sailors.

### Government & Politics
The Free Tribes are governed by a single High Chieftain, which is currently Valdris. Beneath that office, each tribe retains its own internal leadership structure, and those structures vary considerably. Some tribes are led by hereditary chieftains, others by councils of elders, others by whoever has proven themselves most capable in the field. The coalition does not seek to flatten these differences. What Valdris requires from the tribal chieftains is not uniformity but alignment.

In practice, the office of High Chieftain functions less as an administrative role and more as a centre of gravity. There are no written laws, no formal bureaucracy, no courts. Disputes between tribes are brought to Valdris and he resolves them, his judgment carrying the full weight of his personal authority. Coalition-wide decisions, the sanctioning of raids, the direction of shared resources, any dealings with outside parties, are his alone to make. The tribal chieftains are not a council that advises him. They are leaders of their own peoples who have chosen to follow Valdris and his ideals.

The communal living of the Frozen North has not changed under Valdris in its basic principles. The Frozen North's culture of sharing was never a political choice that could be reversed by a new leader. It is a survival mechanism centuries older than the coalition, and it remains the foundation of how resources move between and within tribes. What has changed is direction. The ideology of shared survival has been reoriented toward a shared purpose, from "we share to survive" to "we share to fight".

#### International Relations
The Free Tribes have a only two countries that touch with its borders, and the north cares little for the world beyond their neighbours.

The Free Tribes and Valdyr are neutral, only because the mountain passes to even reach Valdyr are neigh unpassable. However those that have managed to trek through the mountains and enter Valdyr have been met with kindness.

Finally, the Free Tribes and Korvia have a strained relationship. Korvia has not attacked them, has not declared war, hasn't even engaged them enough to earn the dignity of genuine antagonism. The empire is indifferent to them, even while Northerners died on their doorstep. An enemy that fights you, acknowledges that you exist. Korvia's soldiers look through the camps the way a man looks through fog, and it is that blankness, that complete absence of recognition, that has settled into the bones of the Free Tribes as something colder than the freeze itself. Valdris has given that feeling a name and their anger, a direction.

### Economy
The Free Tribes do not have a formal economy in the sense that other nations would recognize. There is no currency, no banking, and no merchant class. Resource distribution is communal, governed by tribal custom rather than market forces.

The primary economic activities are hunting, fishing, and raiding. Though hunting and fishing have been severely disrupted by the freeze, which has killed off or driven away most of the game and sea life the tribes historically depended on. This disruption is the direct cause of the Great Northern Exodus.

Raiding provides the coalition's most reliable external resource income at present, and its strategic importance has grown considerably as hunting has declined. There is a real possibility that within another generation, raiding will be the coalition's only meaningful economic lifeline if the freeze continues.

Trade with other nations is effectively non-existent given Korvia's border posture, and the neigh impassable mountains between the Free Tribes and the Kingdom of Valdyr.
