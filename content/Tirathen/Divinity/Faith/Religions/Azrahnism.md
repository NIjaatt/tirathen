Azrahnism is a religion formed around the worship of the Korvian Empire's Imperial Dragons.

### Basic Info
**Symbol:** Five dragons arranged in a circle around a sword.
**Key People:** Vessar Orin
**Founded in:** Founded by the first Human settlers of Korvia.

### Background
Azrahnism is the state-sponsored religion of the Korvian Empire, built around the veneration of five Imperial Dragons. Draconic beings of immense power and ancient origin who arrived on [[Tirathen]] alongside the Human settlers and have remained willingly bound to Korvia's ruling houses ever since.

To an Azrahnic believer, the Korvian Empire is divinely ordained. Each of its ruling houses sanctified by the dragon that chose to bind itself to their lineage, and the Emperor above them all elevated by the blessing of the Sovereign Dragon itself.

Outside Korvia, Azrahnism is regarded with a mixture of scepticism and unease. The dragons are real enough, their existence is not seriously disputed by anyone with access to credible Korvian sources. However the theology built around them strikes most outsiders as essentially a form of pro-Korvian propoganda. Inside Korvia, such observations is considered both impolite and mildly dangerous to voice.

#### History
The Imperial Dragons did not come to Tirathen as gods, but as companions. Five ancient beings who crossed through the same portal that carried the first Human settlers from their original world. The earliest accounts describe the dragons as having chosen their companions freely, each drawn to a particular human family or leader whose qualities matched their own nature. The Sovereign Dragon to the line that would become the imperial family. The Sea Dragon to the seafarers and navigators who would become House Belthir. The Forest Dragon to the hunters and foresters of what would become House Aerthain. The Underworld Dragon to the scholars and engineers that became House Cassovar. The Sky Dragon to the soldiers and scouts who would become House Valcryn.

In those early decades, the veneration of the dragons was unselfconscious and genuine. People who had crossed between worlds alongside vast and ancient beings and watched them take an interest in the fate of particular families did not need a priesthood to tell them those beings were worth honouring. Shrines appeared and seasonal rites developed naturally. The dragons themselves were not distant or mythologised but present, and occasionally visible. Their relationship with the families they had chosen was understood as mutual rather than simply devotional.

#### Absorption
As Korvia consolidated into an empire and the Baragav line secured its position as the imperial dynasty, the informal faith surrounding the dragons became too useful to leave unmanaged. Official priests were appointed, and doctrines written. The organic relationship between the settlers and their draconic companions was reframed as a divine mandate, the dragons' choices recast not as personal affinities but as sacred endorsements of the ruling order itself.

The formal codification of Azrahnism as a state religion occurred in 1420 P.D, under Emperor Varyn Baragav, who established the first Crown Priesthood and attached it directly to the imperial administration. From that point forward, Azrahnic theology has served two purposes simultaneously: a genuine spiritual framework that many Korvians hold with sincere devotion, and a mechanism for the crown to frame its authority as something beyond ordinary political power.

#### The Corruption
Azrahnic theology was built around five dragons and five houses, a neat symmetry if not for the fact that there was indeed a sixth house, and dragon. The defection of Alaric Rathven, the fifth sword of Korvia shattered the faith in such a way that it has never quite resolved. When the future founder of [[Valdyr]] broke from the first king of Korvia, Athelmyr and claimed independence, he took his dragon's association with him, and the pair became an embarrassment that the crown needed to explain.

The explanation the priesthood developed is the Void Dragon. In the official theology, the Alaric's dragon was corrupted, hollowed out by a malevolent presence the priests call the Void, the sixth dragon whose nature became antithetical to the ordered harmony that the five houses of Korvia represent. The Void Dragon infected the original dragon that had been there and Alaric, turned their loyalty inside out, and used it to fracture the sacred bonds of the Korvian houses.

The Void Dragon serves the Azrahnism's present needs admirably. It explains why Valdyr resists Korvian authority, and provides a religious justification for the long hostility between the two nations. It also gives Azrahnic priests a tidy answer whenever a sincere believer asks the uncomfortable question of why a divinely blessed empire has spent centuries unable to reclaim its own defected territory. The Void Dragon corrupts, and deceives. Its influence makes those under it believe their rebellion is righteous when it is merely poisoned. That Valdyr's people seem perfectly functional and unconvinced of their own corruption is not, in the priesthood's view, evidence against the theory.

### Core Beliefs
#### The Five are Sacred
Azrahnism holds that the five Imperial Dragons represent five essential principles of civilised life, each embodied by the dragon and the house it sanctifies. The Sovereign Dragon embodies authority, the principle that power must have a centre to be meaningful. The Sea Dragon embodies adaptability, the capacity to move through a changing world and return with something valuable. The Forest Dragon embodies patience, the understanding that what is tended carefully outlasts what is consumed quickly. The Underworld Dragon embodies persistence, the willingness to go further than others will to achieve what others cannot. The Sky Dragon, embodies freedom, the understanding at all Humans are free under a united sky.

#### Divine Mandate
The central claim of Azrahnism is that the dragons did not choose their houses arbitrarily. Their choices were expressions of a cosmic order, an alignment between draconic nature and human virtue that sanctifies the ruling houses as the rightful governors of Korvia. To rebel against the Emperor is therefore not merely treason. It is a rejection of the order the dragons chose to uphold. Deeply blasphemous and heretical.

#### The Void Corrupts
The Void Dragon is not a being of power to be feared in the way that the five are to be revered. It is a corrupting influence that enters through pride, defiance, and the rejection of one's ordained place. Korvian priests invoke the Void Dragon in sermons about civil disobedience, foreign influence, and the particular danger of Valdyr's example. A citizen who questions the Emperor's authority is not simply mistaken. They are showing the first signs of the Void's work.

### Organisation
Azrahnism is administered by the Crown Priesthood, a body of priests appointed directly by the Emperor and funded through the imperial treasury. The Crown Priesthood is headed by the Grand Bishop, currently a position held by a senior cleric named Vessar Orin, who advises the Emperor on religious matters and oversees the faith's institutions across all Korvian territory.

Beneath the Grand Bishop, the priesthood is divided into five orders, each dedicated to one of the five Imperial Dragons. Each order maintains its own temples, trains its own priests, and has its own traditions of practice. Temples to the Imperial Dragons are present in every major Korvian city. Smaller shrines exist in most towns, maintained by local priests.

Beyond Korvia's borders, small Azrahnic communities exist primarily among Korvian expatriates, diplomats and merchants. The faith does not actively proselytise abroad, partly because its theology is so specifically tied to Korvian legitimacy that it does not translate easily into a universal message, especially when its core claim is that the Korvian Emperor is divinely sanctioned to rule. The message just doesn't quite resonate with civilians of other nations for some reason.
