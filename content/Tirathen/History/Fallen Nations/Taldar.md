The Kingdom of Taldar was a rich and prideful nation before it's fall. What remains of Taldar in the present day is a name on old maps, a warning in [[Octavian Faith|Octavian]] sermons, and a wound in the land that has not healed in nearly two thousand years.

### Lands
Taldar occupied the region directly north of the [[Halmyr|Holy Kingdom of Halmyr]], bordered to the west by modern day [[Elovan]] and to the east by the [[Dunvaar]] and [[Myrathin]]. Its position made it one of the most important crossroads on the continent. Trade routes from the eastern nations passed through Taldar on their way west, and the peoples of Valloryn moved freely through its roads and cities in an era before Humans had arrived to reshape the political order.

In the present day, those roads are dust and those cities are ruin. The locals and cartographers of the current age have long since stopped calling the region Taldar, instead referring to it as the Badlands, a name that carries no history, only a warning. The land beyond the [[Gates of Ikanos]] is warped and hostile, the sky above carrying a permanent unnatural cast. Demonic lords have claimed the ruins of Taldari cities as their seats of power. Yet the Gates of Ikanos stand ever tall and mighty, unyielding to the darkness.

### Culture
Taldar in its time was as diverse as its location demanded. Dwarves, Orcs, Beastfolk, and dozens of peoples of all ancestries passed through or settled within its borders, and Taldari culture was shaped by centuries of that traffic. Taldar was known for commerce, mediation, and for the particular cosmopolitan characters that form when many peoples share the same roads long enough to stop seeing each other as strangers.

This is all, of course, speculation. What survived of Taldar's people were wiped out when the Humans arrived, erased by the [[The Spellplague|Spellplague]]. What fragments still exist are held by Dunvaar archivists and a handful of Halmyran scholars, though the Octavian Faith has little interest in preserving the memory of a nation they use primarily as a lesson.

### The Darkrift
In 945 P.D, a witch in service to a demon lord tore open a rift into the demonic planes within Taldari territory. The kingdom fell within years, overrun before any coordinated response could be mounted. Western Myrathin was already being swallowed by the time the First Crusade was formally named in 961 P.D.

The crusaders of Halmyr, joined by forces from Dunvaar, Valdyr, Myrathin, and what little remained of Taldari forces, pushed the demonic tide back within the borders of Taldar and held the line, but could not close the rift. Pope Ikanos deemed the recovery of Taldar impossible, and made the tough decision to seal the land forever. The Gates of Ikanos, great wardstones forged by Dunvaaran hands, carved by Elven craft, and consecrated with Halmyran divine power, were placed along every border of Taldar. They hold to the present day.

The Gates allow entry, but do not allow exit. Whatever passes through the Gates of Ikanos into Badlands of Taldar does not come back out.

### Present Day
Over the course of two thousand years, the name of Taldar has been lost to those unstudied in history. Most know the region Taldar occupied as the [[Badlands]] in the present era. Halmyr does not speak of Taldar often, but when it does, it speaks loudly. The kingdom is the Faith's most durable cautionary text, proof of what demonic corruption can do to a nation that fails to guard itself, and of what it costs to contain it once it takes root. The Gates of Ikanos appear in sermons, in children's instruction, in the formal justifications for the Ministry of Prosecution's vigilance against heresy. Taldar is the answer to the question of why such vigilance is necessary.

However, Halmyr has other uses for Taldar. Those convicted of the gravest offences against the Octavian Faith, crimes that would otherwise earn death, are instead offered a choice. They are brought to the Gates of Ikanos and told that if they can find a way to close the Darkrift, their crimes will be considered expunged and their souls considered clean. None have returned, and none are expected to.

There are others who enter Taldar by choice rather than sentence. Adventurers who believe the ruins of old Taldari cities hold relics from before the Spellplague, scholars convinced that understanding the Darkrift requires studying it directly, mercenaries hired by collectors whose desire for demonic artefacts exceeds their good sense. The Gates make no distinction between the condemned and the curious. All who enter do so on the same terms. Once you enter, you cannot leave unless death takes you.
